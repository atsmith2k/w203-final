Annotated Bibliography

Primary Sources:

  1. Seltzer, Thomas, Horatio Winslow, Max Eastman, and Theodore Watts., “The Masses, January 1913.” A Monthly Magazine Devoted to the Interests of the Working People, no. 4, January 1913.

    The Masses Magazine was produced as a source of propaganda and promoted radical ideas that were against the first World War. The images used are very dark and are specifically used to scare the reader. The purpose of this magazine was to insight fear and sway the American people to be against the war efforts. This magazine promoted the anti-draft effort.

  2. Artcraft Pictures Corporation, The Little American, 1917.

    The Little American was a WW1 propaganda film produced in 1917. The story is of Angela More, a survivor of a German U-Boat attack where she ends up stranded in her family's homeland of France. The film held controversy about whether it should be shown because of the United State's declaration of war against Germany early in 1917. After a trip through court, the jury ruled that the film was allowed to be shown in Chicago, despite the fears of a riot.

  3. “America in Europe: a Paper Published in the Interest of Good Fellowship among All Nations.”, America in Europe. July 29, 1918.

    This German propaganda newspaper was distributed by air in 1918 to the American troops on the ground. The newspaper was produced to insight fear and propaganda in the eyes of American Soldiers who were overseas. The newspaper cites excerpts from George Washington to incite further confusion about what the US is fighting for. It was Axis powers propaganda against the Allied Powers.


Secondary Sources:

  1. Andersen, Robin., A Century of Media, a Century of War., New York: Peter Lang, 2007.

    This book by Robin Anderson gives a historical timeline of the history of war and media. The books give a good depiction of media strategies used to manipulate the political climate of war, including propaganda. Also, A Century of Media, a Century of War, gives the reader a reference to how media encouraged the way wars are fought, through censorship and propaganda.

  2. Needham, Matthew Edward., “War of the Words: Propaganda, Public Opinion, and Revolutionary Mexico during the Great War (CHAPTER 3: PROPAGANDA AND WAR REPORTING).”, Dissertation, ProQuest, 2017.

    Needham's dissertation is an account of the propaganda, public opinion, and revolutionary Mexico during the Great War. It describes the propaganda in Mexico through the media and the way the powers were portrayed.

  3. Fife, Thomas William., “AFRICAN-AMERICANS IN THE GREAT WAR 1917-19.” Military Images 27, no. 4 (2006): 4–46.

    In this Journal Entry, Fife gives an account of the experience endured by African Americans who served in the Great War. With the hardship that African American's experienced in the United States, it was a large effort by the media to encourage that the war was, "...a Black American's war, just as well as white America's."

  4. Leadingham, Norma Ruth Compton., “Propaganda and Poetry during the Great War.” Thesis, ProQuest, 2008.

    The thesis digs into the British government's role in propaganda, specifically that of them utilizing poetry. Leadingham goes on to give vivid detail of poems which show an example of these wartime propaganda usages.

  5. Osselaer, Heidi J.,Family. Arizona's Deadliest Gunfight: Draft Resistance and Tragedy at the Power Cabin, 1918. Norman: University of Oklahoma Press, 2018.

    The book is a historical account of Arizona's deadliest shoot out that was caused over a dispute between draft dodging. It shows the effects that the war and the draft have on the population. The book also displays the federal government's enforcement of the draft.

  6. Whelan, Bernadette. “American Propaganda and Ireland during World War One: the Work of the Committee on Public Information.” Irish Studies Review 25, no. 2 (2017): 141–69.

    Whelan gives an overview of the Committee on Public Information (CPI) which was the first established propaganda division in US History. It tells how the Axis and Allied powers combatted propaganda during the Great War. The Article summarized President Wilson's participation in media propaganda during war times and how the effort was fought in homeland United States.

  7. Benson, Krystina. “Archival Analysis of The Committee on Public Information: The Relationship between Propaganda, Journalism, and Popular Culture.” International Journal of Technology, Knowledge & Society 6, no. 4 (2010): 151–64.

    This article analyzes the United States government's strategy to persuade the American public into war. A broad overview of the injection of propaganda into media is included throughout the entry. As shown in the title, the entry studies the relationship between propaganda, journalism, and popular culture using the Committee on Public Information as a case study.

Peer Reviewed Sources:

  1. Magee, Gayle. “‘She's a Dear Old Lady’: English Canadian Popular Songs from World War I.” American Music 34, no. 4 (2016): 474–506.

    Magee's Journal Article highlights the fracture of cultural and racial perspectives through pre and post-war Canada. It displays this through the analysis of songs and culture pieces that were released during the Great War. The article stresses this Canadian cultural divide by blaming the 1911 census for requiring origin nationality to not allow "Canadian".

  2. Fischer, Nick. “The Committee on Public Information and the Birth of US State Propaganda.” Australasian Journal of American Studies 35, no. 1 (July 2016): 51–78.

    The article gives an overview of the birth of propaganda in the United States. It shows the government initiative of propaganda during the Great War. Fischer displays propaganda pieces and reflects on their effects on the population's opinion and media involvement.

  3. Tune, Tanfer Emin. “Less Sugar, More Warships: Food as American Propaganda in the First World War.” War in History 19, no. 2 (April 2012): 193–216.

    The article gives an overview of the introduction of food being used as a form of propaganda in the First World War. The propaganda strategies described in the articles inspired the mobilization of the middle-class American woman. The article displays food-driven propaganda with the 'Food Will Win the War!' poster; the poster was meant to re-educate the public on food production and consumption.
